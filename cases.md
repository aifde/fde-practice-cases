## Selection standard

This edition intentionally reduces the library from 30 cases to 10. A case remains only when public evidence identifies a real organization, a specific AI-enabled workflow, a production or released system, meaningful implementation work, and observable adoption or outcomes.

The cases are AI FDE-style because they join domain discovery, hands-on systems integration, model and workflow design, evaluation, governance, change management, and production learning. The label describes the work, not necessarily the formal job title used by the participating companies.

Each full case contains:

- an evidence summary and confidence statement;
- the operational problem and delivery approach;
- a production workflow diagram;
- integration, evaluation, security, and human-control details available publicly;
- reported outcomes with careful attribution;
- a section explaining what remains unproven;
- mapping to the [OpenAI and Amazon FDE role framework](role-requirements.md);
- at least three public references.

## Cross-case delivery patterns

1. **Start from an operational decision.** The strongest teams map who acts, what information they need, where delay or risk occurs, and which outcome matters before selecting a model.
2. **Build inside the existing workflow.** Successful systems appear in hospital operations, security review, advisor tools, enterprise workspaces, support channels, legal drafting, and business applications instead of requiring a separate generic chat product.
3. **Connect context and actions.** Production value depends on identity, permissions, business data, retrieval, APIs, ticketing, account actions, and audit trails.
4. **Design the human boundary.** Clinical decisions, financial advice, security remediation, disputed payments, infrastructure diagnosis, and legal approval retain explicit expert control.
5. **Evaluate the real task.** Cases measure retrieval quality, answer acceptance, resolution time, repeat contact, adoption, or workflow outcomes. General model benchmarks are not enough.
6. **Treat rollout as engineering.** Training, champions, office hours, joint workshops, policy authoring, support, and quarterly releases are part of the delivered system.
7. **Revise the automation boundary.** Klarna's later service changes show why launch metrics cannot replace ongoing quality evidence.
8. **Turn one deployment into a reusable platform.** Shared evaluation suites, retrieval components, governance patterns, operating procedures, and release mechanisms make subsequent workflows faster.

## Evidence note

Deployment facts and metrics are attributed to their publishers. Participant case studies are useful primary evidence of what was built, but they can be selective. Regulatory filings, customer publications, product documentation, and independent reporting are included where available. The research was refreshed in July 2026; readers should recheck live sources before relying on a metric, architecture, or job requirement.
